## TRANSLATION APPROACH
This translation treats the Heart Sutra as a poem of radical transformation rather than a philosophical treatise. The negations become incantatory, building like waves crashing against the shore of illusion. The paradox of form and emptiness is rendered through imagery of reflection and dissolution—water mirroring moonlight, foam dissolving back into sea—suggesting identity rather than mere equivalence.

---

## FULL TRANSLATION

### The Heart of the Perfection of Wisdom

---

**Avalokiteshvara**

Avalokiteshvara, Bodhisattva of boundless compassion,
drifted into the depths of Prajñāpāramitā—
where suddenly he saw:
the five skandhas,
like morning mist,
were empty.

And in that seeing,
suffering fell away.

---

**The Mirror of Form**

Form is emptiness.
Emptiness is form.
Not two—
not form leading to emptiness,
nor emptiness becoming form.

Form is not other than emptiness.
Emptiness is not other than form.

What you call "form"
bears the very signature of void.
What you call "void"
wears the flesh and bone of form.

So too with sensation, perception, impulse, consciousness—
all five, the same translucent song.

---

**Shariputra**

Listen, Shariputra:
all phenomena bear this single mark—
emptiness.

They do not arise.
They do not cease.
Neither stained nor stainless.
Neither growing nor diminishing.

---

**The Great Unweaving**

Therefore in emptiness—
no form, no feeling, no thought, no will, no knowing;
no eye, no ear, no nose, no tongue, no body, no mind;
no shape, no sound, no scent, no taste, no touch, no thing;
no realm of sight, no realm of mind...

no ignorance,
no end of ignorance,
no aging, no death,
no end of aging and death;

no suffering, no cause, no cessation, no path;
no wisdom, no attainment, no non-attainment.

---

**The Liberation**

Because there is nothing to attain,
the Bodhisattva dwells in Prajñāpāramitā,
heart without hindrance.

Without hindrance—no fear.

Far beyond all confused imagining,
she enters the stillness
of final Nirvana.

---

**The Buddhas**

All Buddhas of the three times—
past, present, future—
awaken to unsurpassed, perfect enlightenment
through this very Prajñāpāramitā.

---

**The Mantra**

Therefore know this:
the mantra of Prajñāpāramitā,
the mantra of great wisdom,
the unsurpassed mantra,
the equal-to-none mantra,
the mantra that silences all suffering—
is true, not false.

*Gate gate pāragate pārasaṃgate bodhi svāhā.*

*Gone, gone,
gone beyond,
gone altogether beyond—
O awakening,
what wonder!*

---

## KEY TRANSLATION CHOICES

- **śūnyatā**: "Emptiness" — I keep the standard translation but render it through natural imagery (mist, foam, sea) to prevent nihilistic misreading. The emptiness here is luminous, not bleak.

- **prajñāpāramitā**: "Perfection of Wisdom" — I retain this as the title but sometimes let it resonate untranslated, treating it as the name of an ocean the bodhisattva enters.

- **"Form is emptiness, emptiness is form"**: Rendered as "Not two— / not form leading to emptiness, / nor emptiness becoming form." The negative formulation emphasizes that this is not a causal relationship but an ontological identity. The line breaks create pauses for the paradox to land.

- **"Gate gate pāragate..."**: Translated as "Gone, gone, / gone beyond, / gone altogether beyond— / O awakening, / what wonder!" The repetition builds momentum like footsteps disappearing into distance. "Svāhā" becomes "what wonder" to capture its sense of completion and awe, rather than the liturgical "hail" or "so be it."

- **The negations (no eye, no ear...)**: Structured with semicolons and line breaks to create a cascading, dismantling rhythm. The reader should feel the world dissolving, not as loss, but as liberation. The sequence follows the traditional progression from skandhas to sense bases to elements of dependent origination.

---

## WHAT IS EMPHASIZED/LOST

**Emphasized:**
- The incantatory, transformative power of the text as oral poetry meant to be chanted
- The paradoxical identity of form and emptiness through natural imagery
- The emotional arc: from perception to liberation to wonder
- The mantra as a journey into disappearance and emergence

**Obscured:**
- Technical Abhidharma precision (the detailed enumeration of dharmas is compressed)
- The philosophical shock value of absolute negation (softened by poetic imagery)
- Specific doctrinal categories (I render "dependent origination" elements more gently)
- The liturgical gravitas of "svāhā" (my "what wonder" captures awe but loses ritual resonance)

This translation invites the reader to *feel* the sutra's wisdom rather than merely understand it—the difference between reading about water and tasting it.
