## TRANSLATION APPROACH
My translation seeks to render the Pali as living poetry—flowing verses that carry the reader downstream through the nested conditional chains like water finding its course. I prioritize the musicality of the Buddha's teaching, allowing repetition to become incantation and technical terms to unfold as images rather than definitions. The goal is to evoke the feeling of sitting in Añjana Wood, listening to wisdom unfurl like leaves in wind.

---

## FULL TRANSLATION

### The Setting

At Sāketa, in the Deer Park of Añjana Wood,
where shadows of ancient trees dapple the earth,
there dwelt the Blessed One.

Then came Kuṇḍaliya, the wandering one,
a pilgrim of gardens, a sojourner of sacred groves,
seeking shade beneath the Bodhi tree's kin.

---

### The Question

*"I am a wanderer of parks, Master Gotama,
a breeze that drifts from grove to grove,
breathing the fragrance of many teachings.

But tell me—what garden do you tend?
What blooms in the soil of your practice?"

---

### The Answer

*"The Tathāgata gardens for a single harvest:
Knowledge and Liberation—the fruit and the flowering,
the knowing that sets the heart free."

---

### The Chain Unfolds

**First Ring:**
*"What waters, what sunlight, what tending
cultivates this garden of Knowledge and Liberation?"

*"Seven blossoms, Kuṇḍaliya—
Seven Factors of Awakening,
deeply rooted, abundantly grown."

---

**Second Ring:**
*"And what soil, what rain, what patient cultivation
brings these seven blossoms to fullness?"

*"Four Foundations of Mindfulness,
Kuṇḍaliya—
like four watchful guardians
at the gates of perception."

---

**Third Ring:**
*"What path leads to these four guardians?
What practice opens their doors?"

*"Three Ways of Beautiful Conduct,
Kuṇḍaliya—
body, speech, and mind
walking in harmony."

---

**Fourth Ring:**
*"And where does Beautiful Conduct begin?
What is its source, its spring, its first light?"

*"Restraint of the Senses, Kuṇḍaliya—
the gentle guardian at the threshold of perception."

---

### The Living of It

*How does one dwell in Sense Restraint?
How does this guardian keep watch?*

Here, Kuṇḍaliya, is how:

When the eye meets form—
a face, a flower, a flicker of beauty—
let it not become a hook in the heart.
Do not chase it with grasping,
do not let craving bloom in its shadow.

The body remains still as a mountain pool.
The heart rests undisturbed,
well-gathered, well-freed—
like a bird that sees the net
and simply does not land.

---

### The River Returns to Itself

Thus cultivated, Sense Restraint
flows into Beautiful Conduct.

Thus cultivated, Beautiful Conduct
flows into the Four Foundations.

Thus cultivated, the Four Foundations
flow into the Seven Factors.

Thus cultivated, the Seven Factors
flow into Knowledge and Liberation—
the garden where the heart
finally comes home to itself.

---

### The Conversion

Hearing these words,
Kuṇḍaliya the wanderer
felt something open in his chest
like morning glories greeting the sun.

*"Marvelous, Master Gotama! Marvelous!
Like one who reveals what was hidden,
who lights a lamp in the darkness—
you have made the path visible.

I take refuge in you, Blessed One.
I take refuge in the Dhamma.
I take refuge in the Sangha.

Remember me, Lord,
as one who has crossed the threshold,
from this day forward,
a disciple at your feet."

---

## KEY TRANSLATION CHOICES

- **bojjhaṅgā**: "Factors of Awakening" — I retain this standard rendering for clarity, but frame them as "blossoms" in the poetic imagery to evoke their organic, unfolding nature.

- **satipaṭṭhānā**: "Foundations of Mindfulness" — translated literally, then expanded metaphorically as "four watchful guardians at the gates of perception" to capture both their stabilizing and protective functions.

- **indriyasaṁvara**: "Restraint of the Senses / Sense Restraint" — rendered as "the gentle guardian at the threshold of perception" to soften any harsh connotation of repression, emphasizing instead a wise, welcoming vigilance.

- **vijjāvimutti**: "Knowledge and Liberation" — kept as a paired phrase to preserve the intimacy of knowing (vijjā) and the freedom (vimutti) that emerges from it. They are not separate achievements but fruit and flowering of the same root.

- **ṭhitaṁ cittaṁ**: "steady mind / heart rests undisturbed" — I chose "heart" over "mind" to honor the emotional resonance of the phrase, and paired it with the image of a mountain pool to suggest both stillness and depth.

---

## WHAT IS EMPHASIZED/LOST

**What is emphasized:**
- The cascading, river-like quality of the conditional chains—the sense that each practice naturally *flows into* the next rather than mechanically causing it
- The organic imagery (garden, blossoms, water, light) to convey that awakening is a natural process, not a manufactured achievement
- The emotional transformation in Kuṇḍaliya—the wonder, the opening, the sense of finally finding home
- The gentleness of restraint—presented as wisdom rather than austerity

**What may be obscured:**
- The technical precision of Pali terms (some nuances of abhihaṃsati, for instance, are softened)
- The formal, repetitive structure that in the original has a doctrinal, almost legal quality
- The specific mechanics of each Factor of Awakening (only the general structure is preserved)
- The masculine grammatical markers (bhikkhu, bhavaṃ) which I rendered inclusively

This translation chooses the felt experience of the teaching over its systematic exposition, hoping to move the reader toward practice through beauty rather than obligation through structure.
